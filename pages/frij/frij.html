FRIJ

FRIJ was conceived and designed as part of the Columbia Engineering UX/UI Design Boot Camp course by myself and three other junior designers.

Design Challenge: Create an app that solves an everyday problem.


Timeline: 3 weeks


Deliverables: High fidelity design mockup & design process documentation


Tools Used: Figma, Adobe Color, Google Docs, Google Forms, Trello


USER RESEARCH


At the beginning of the project, our team spent time brainstorming everyday problems. One issue that all of us had been running into lately was food waste; we all had lost food to spoilage due to crowded fridges, bad cooking habits, or plain forgetfulness. We decided to design an app that would keep track of a household's food inventory, expiration dates, and shopping list.

With our goal set, we put together a proto-persona to organize our needs, pain points, and assumptions.



To find out more about our potential users, we wrote and distributed a survey on Google Forms that asked respondents about their grocery-related habits, including:

  • Household size
  • Whether they share groceries and shopping responsibilities with roommates
  • Whether they use a grocery list for shopping (physical or digital)
  • What factors contribute to food spoiling in their household

More than half of respondents make a grocery list, and more than 75% reported throwing food out at least twice a month.

As well as the survey, we conducted several one-on-one interviews asking more in-depth questions about grocery shopping timing, process, and frustrations.


We learned about a myriad of ways people keep track of their grocery list. We also found that most respondents shop for their housemates or family as well as themselves, and that most respondents also throw away perishables. Over 90% of survey respondents said they would want to be able to see a list of their whole household food inventory, which assured our team that this app was worth designing.


IDEATION


Through our research, we found that most people wanted to reduce their food waste, but ran into obstacles including time constraints, forgetting what food they have already, and the allure of ordering takeout.


PROBLEM STATEMENT


How might we help people feel that grocery planning and shopping are exciting, simple, and fun, and help them feel confident in their ability to build better habits in terms of reducing their food waste?


At this point, we conducted a competitor analysis and found that most existing grocery list apps were designed for single users and only had simple features like keeping a list of text strings. Our team realized this was a good opportunity to explore adding social features. We synthesized all our research findings into an affinity map and organized it all with an 'I Like, I Wish, What If' framework to define our features to build out.



Because most grocery list apps are for single users, we focused on two social features in particular:

  • A shared shopping list accessible to all members of a household
  • A scoreboard where a user and their friends are ranked by a 'sustainability score' based on how much food they have used before expiry

Our goal was to elevate our app from a simple grocery list to an app that would foster a sense of community between users and the people they shared food with by sharing the responsibility of grocery shopping, as well as between households through friendly competition in sustainability.


We also saw potential in Apple's photographic text recognition feature to make it easy to add items to the household inventory by simply taking a photo or screenshot of a grocery receipt.

With some ideas for screen layouts between us, we next collaborated on creating a user flow chart to help us build the fundamentals of our user interface.


The user flow chart helped us figure out the critical functions we needed to start putting an interface together:

  • Grocery list - add/delete items
  • Receipt Scanner - add items to grocery list with receipt photo
  • Fridge - inventory of groceries in different storage areas
  • User Profile - account creation/login, friends list, sustainability scoreboard

We built our mid-fi prototype off our base of research and planning, settling on a cool blue tone as the primary color identity for our app. We also decided to use a simple five-point bottom navigation bar for this iteration.


TESTING & ITERATING


We conducted five user tests with our prototype. Some things we found out were that:

  • Users were able to complete the simple navigation test we devised
  • Users were curious about the scoreboard feature but didn't quite understand it
  • Users weren't aware they could scroll the icons in the storage area selection screen
  • Users didn't like being prompted to log in right away
  • Users wanted the app to suggest or save recipes

For the next iteration, we also wanted to work on the brand identity of our app. We settled on the name FRIJ and my colleague Adi Chawla drew up a logo and designed some iconography for the menus. With our branding and the user insights from our testing, we built an improved high fidelity prototype.


With this prototype, we made a lot of adjustments, including:

  • Redesigned home page and dashboard
  • Color-coded visual indicators for upcoming and passed expiry dates
  • Customizable storage space
  • Redesigned overlays for grocery items


CONCLUSIONS


Our team was happy with what we built in the three weeks of this project. However, we did have a list of further considerations, had we had more time to work on it, including:

  • Additional user testing
  • Personalized food waste journey showing environmental impact
  • More in-depth sorting and filtering features for inventory
  • Build out features for household list sharing
  • Money management features
  • Food prep calendar
  • Recipe suggestions based on diet and inventory
