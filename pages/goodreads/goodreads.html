GOODREADS REDESIGN

This redesign of Goodreads was a group project completed as part of the Columbia Engineering UX/UI Design Boot Camp in 2023.


Design Challenge: Redesign a website


Timeline: 3 weeks


Deliverables: High fidelity design mockup & design process documentation


Tools Used: Figma, Adobe Color, Photoshop, Google Docs, Google Forms, Trello


RESEARCH


For this project, three colleagues and I were tasked with choosing an existing website and conducting a redesign of the user interface. As a quartet of literature nerds, we decided to focus on Goodreads.com, a popular site where users can rate and review books. The site was launched in 2007 and acquired by Amazon in 2013, and today has some 90 million members.

Our first move was to create a proto-persona to help us organize our users' demographics, goals, and pain points. It also aided us in developing interview and survey questions.


Our prototypical user is a young professional who wants to read more but doesn't have many people in his life with whom to recommend and discuss books. With this persona in mind we wrote and distributed a survey that asked about peoples' habits around reading.


We also conducted five one-on-one interviews to get a more in-depth picture of someone's habits and their personal experience using Goodreads as it currently exists.


Some of our research findings include:

  • About half of people prefer reading physical books, with 20% preferring e-books and 15% preferring audiobooks
  • Almost everyone said they wished they were reading more
  • People were not aware of most community features on Goodreads due to the user interface
  • Most people chose books to read based on recommendations from friends and reviews on the internet

We also conducted a heuristic evaluation of the existing Goodreads site

From this we found:

  • The main navigation bars lack links to social features
  • The 'reviews' section of a book page is many pages long and blocks the user from finding social features
  • Every item on the sign-in page has a whole paragraph of explanation, overwhelming new users
  • Book pages have few items relating to the book in question, are too focused on linking to other works or outside store pages

DEFINITION & IDEATION


PROBLEM STATEMENT


Goodreads' mission is "help people find and share books they love." Users want to use the site to share discussion and recommendations with like-minded readers, but the site is currently difficult to navigate and the overall design hides helpful features and fails to keep users engaged.


From our user research and personal experience using Goodreads, we knew that a lot of features on the site were either missing or so difficult to navigate to that users had no idea they even existed. Next, we plotted out a user journey map to help us solidify the type of user experience we wanted to design for. This map is for a young woman in her 20s who wants to read more, makes an account on Goodreads, and finds a community of people to share her reading journey with, leading to long-term engagement on the site.



With a solid idea of what we wanted to improve about the existing site, and how we wanted users to use our new interface, we brainstormed features and organized our ideas with a feature prioritization matrix to decide what to build out for our prototype.



Next, we made a user flow chart to map out the features for our prototype. We thought it would be important for users to be able to sign in, search for books, see a book page, and navigate a discussion forum page.



WIREFRAMING & TESTING & STYLING


Our first prototype consisted of a half-dozen of the most important pages that our user flow covered, including the home page, search results, book page, and discussion page. We wanted the community features to be more prominent, so we added links to discussion posts closer to the tops of pages; the home screen will take the user straight to the latest discussion they posted in, and the home page for a specific book has user reviews right next to the latest active discussion topics for that book.


Our first prototype with our navigable wireframe went through three usability tests. We asked our testers to sign in and navigate to certain pages. We found:

  • Users were interested in the community features but were not sure why they would want to log in
  • Users were overwhelmed by the information density of the pages
  • Users liked the 'recommend to a friend' button
  • Users had concerns about getting spoiled by looking up a book they had not finished
  • Users were more interested in features related to reading and discussing books over features related to purchasing books


We also investigated the accessibility and aesthetics of the current Goodreads site when deciding how to style it. The current site is reasonably accessible, and we decided not to change the typeface or color scheme, as our redesign was more focused on the layout and interface. We did, of course, create a style tile to guide our design for the higher fidelity prototype.



ITERATIONS & CONCLUSIONS


With our user feedback and style guide, we commenced work on the next iteration. For each page in our wireframe, we created a higher fidelity version in accordance with our style guide, as well as with our user feedback.

While we moved on from this project after a few weeks, there is still plenty more that could be done. Here are some of the steps we could have taken with more time and resources:

  • Keep building out social features like friend groups and customizable fields
  • Approach redesign from mobile-first framework
  • Contact Goodreads' stakeholders to get feedback from them
  • Conduct A/B tests on UI features to enhance usability
  • Design fun features like year-end wrap-up for users, popularity timelines
